Cadences

A cadence is the ending of a phrase in music.

It is similar to a full stop, comma or an exclamation mark at the end of a sentence.

The reason of a cadence is to create a sense of resolution or finality in the music.

  • Perfect cadence

    • A perfect cadence ends from a chord V to a chord I.

  • Imperfect cadence

    • An imperfect cadence can either end from a chord I to a chord V, a chord II to a chord V, or a chord IV to a chord V.

  • Interrupted cadence

    • An interrupted cadence ends from a chord V to a chord VI. This changes the key from a major to a minor.

  • Plagal cadence

    • A plagal cadence ends from a chord IV to a chord I. This is also known as the 'Amen cadence' due to its frequent use in church music.

Cadences appear in many musical styles, but their use and sound can vary.

  • Classical Music

    • In classical music, cadences follow traditional rules and are used to clearly mark the ends of phrases, sections or pieces.
    Perfect and plagal cadences are especially common.

  • Jazz

    • In jazz, cadences can be more fluid and less predictable. Musicians often use extended chords and substitutions, leading to more complex cadences.
    Jazz musicians may also use modal interchange, borrowing chords from different keys.

  • Pop Music

    • In pop music, cadences are often straightforward and catchy. Perfect cadences are frequently used to create a sense of resolution at the end of a chorus or verse.
    Pop music often relies on simple chord progressions, making cadences easy to recognize.

  • Folk Music

    • In folk music, cadences can be more varied and may not always follow traditional rules.
    Folk musicians often use simple chord structures, and cadences may be less pronounced, blending into the overall melody.
